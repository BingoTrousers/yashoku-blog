*Yakitori* means grilled bird. The word has come to mean almost exclusively chicken, though a serious yakitori counter will serve offal, cartilage, and parts of the animal that most cuisines do not regard as food at all. What makes it interesting is not any individual cut but the accumulated logic of the format: the charcoal, the rotation, the particular combination of high heat and proximity to the cook.

A yakitori chef tends between forty and sixty skewers at any given moment. The bed of charcoal — *binchotan*, a hardwood variety from Wakayama Prefecture that burns extremely hot and clean, with almost no smoke — radiates heat that can be calibrated by the cook's distance from it. The skewers are rotated and repositioned continuously, each at a different stage of its cook. The process requires sustained attention over four or five hours of service every night, which is why the best yakitori chefs are not doing anything else simultaneously.

## The Cuts

A proper yakitori menu is a catalogue of the bird. The most famous cuts:

*Negima* — alternating pieces of thigh meat and spring onion, the original skewer and still the most ordered. The fat from the thigh bastes the onion as it renders; the onion softens and sweetens. Neither ingredient would be as good alone.

*Momo* — thigh meat only, basted in *tare* sauce (a dark reduction of soy, mirin, and sake that accumulates complexity as the night's drippings are added to the pot) or salted (*shio*) for those who prefer the meat's flavour without the sauce's sweetness.

*Kawa* — chicken skin, coiled tightly on the skewer and grilled until it blisters and crackles. The rendering is long and the result is essentially a crisp. It is the cut that separates people who understand yakitori from people who don't.

*Tsukune* — minced chicken shaped around the skewer, grilled until just set, dipped in raw egg yolk at the table. A good tsukune is soft and yielding, seasoned assertively, with a slight crust from the charcoal that gives way immediately.

*Reba* — liver, which requires the most precise cooking of any cut. The interior must remain pink; grilled through, liver becomes chalky and loses the iron-rich mineral quality that makes it worth eating. At a counter where the chef knows what they are doing, reba is the order that proves it.

*Hatsu* — heart, dense and muscular, with a stronger flavour than thigh and a texture that rewards the second bite more than the first. *Sunagimo* — gizzard — snaps cleanly under the tooth and has almost no fat. *Nankotsu* — cartilage, from the chest — is crunchy throughout. These are the cuts that the chef eats after service.

## Tare vs Shio

The question the waiter will ask when you order — *tare* or *shio*? — is the fundamental yakitori decision. *Tare* (sauce) adds sweetness, depth, and a glaze that caramelises on the second pass over the charcoal. *Shio* (salt) leaves the meat exposed and reveals what the chicken actually tastes like, which at a counter using properly raised birds is a different thing from supermarket chicken.

The standard guidance is to use shio for delicate or high-quality cuts — thigh, breast, soft cartilage — and tare for richer cuts like skin or for tsukune, where the sauce's sweetness complements the mince's fat. In practice, experienced eaters develop preferences that do not always follow this logic, and good yakitori chefs will accommodate specific requests without comment.

## Where to Eat It

Yakitori exists on a spectrum from the small *tachi-nomi* (standing bars) near train stations — where a skewer costs 100 yen and the beer is served in cans — to dedicated counter restaurants in Shinjuku and Ginza where dinner for two runs past 20,000 yen.

In the mid-range: the Yurakucho area beneath the elevated train tracks in central Tokyo has had yakitori restaurants in the arches under the tracks since the early postwar period. The setting — low ceilings, smoke, trains passing overhead every few minutes — is unchanged, and some of the shops have been in continuous operation for over sixty years. The chicken is not the absolute best in the city. The experience of eating it there cannot be replicated at a newer, more expensive place.

---

Yakitori is a form that rewards patience — in the cook, who is doing one thing for four hours with full attention, and in the eater, who should order slowly, eat deliberately, and not try to finish the menu in one sitting. The correct pace is two skewers, conversation, two more skewers, a refill. The charcoal is still burning.
