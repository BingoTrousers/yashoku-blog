Kanazawa sits on the Sea of Japan coast in Ishikawa Prefecture, at the foot of the Noto Peninsula, and spent most of the Edo period as the wealthiest domain in Japan outside of Edo itself. The Maeda clan, who governed the city for four centuries, patronised arts and crafts with unusual enthusiasm — ceramics, lacquerware, silk dyeing, noh theatre — and, as a consequence, ate extremely well. When the Meiji Restoration reorganised Japan's administrative map, Kanazawa shrank in political importance. What it kept was the food.

The city is frequently compared to Kyoto — both were spared the bombing of the Second World War, both have intact historic architecture, both have food cultures that prioritise seasonal restraint over spectacle. The comparison is fair but undersells Kanazawa slightly. Where Kyoto's cuisine is shaped by centuries of Buddhist abstinence and imperial formality, Kanazawa's is shaped by proximity to the Sea of Japan's cold, deep waters and a seafood abundance that Kyoto cannot match from its landlocked position.

## Omicho Market

The centre of Kanazawa's food culture is **Omicho Ichiba**, a covered market that has operated in some form since the eighteenth century and that currently houses around 180 shops selling seafood, vegetables, tofu, pickles, and prepared food under a series of connected glass-roofed arcades.

Omicho in the early morning is one of the better places to understand what Japanese market culture looks like away from the tourist-managed experience of Tsukiji's outer market or Nishiki in Kyoto. The vendors here are selling primarily to restaurants and local households, not to visitors, and the pacing is different. In winter, snow crab arrives in spectacular volume — stacked in tanks or displayed on ice along the seafood row — and the market takes on an atmosphere of serious commerce that is interesting to observe even if you are not buying.

The market has a cluster of sushi and *donburi* restaurants on its upper floor that open at lunch. They are good, mostly aimed at visitors, and the set menus are well priced for the quality. The more useful thing to do at Omicho, at any hour, is simply to walk through it, watch what is moving, and buy something small from a stall to eat on the spot.

## What the Sea Provides

The cold, deep waters of the Sea of Japan produce seafood that the Pacific side of Honshu cannot easily replicate, and Kanazawa's restaurants have built their identity around a handful of species that arrive at exceptional quality.

*Nodoguro* — literally "black throat," a rosy sea bass (*Doederleinia berycoides*) — is the prestige ingredient, a fish with unusually high fat content that makes it closer in texture to toro tuna than to lean white fish. It is grilled with salt, steamed, or served as sashimi, and in the last decade it has become expensive enough that the city's restaurant industry has begun branding itself around it in the way that Kobe brands itself around beef.

*Zuwaigani* — snow crab — runs from November through March and transforms the city. Restaurants that operate at a reasonable price point in summer become difficult to book in crab season. The local specialty is *kano-zuwaigani*, the female snow crab, which is small but carries intensely flavoured eggs — *uchiko* and *sotoko*, inner and outer roe — that are considered the finest part of the animal by people who know what they are ordering.

## Jibu-ni

If Nagoya has miso katsu and Kyoto has kaiseki, Kanazawa has *jibu-ni* — a simmered dish that is one of the better arguments for regional Japanese cooking as a serious form.

The dish involves duck or chicken dredged in wheat flour or arrowroot starch before being simmered in a light dashi broth, which causes the coating to dissolve into the liquid and thicken it around the meat. It is served with *fu* (wheat gluten), seasonal vegetables, and wasabi. The name is thought to derive from *jibu jibu*, an onomatopoeia for the sound the dish makes while simmering.

The flour-coating technique is characteristic of Kanazawa cooking in a way that points to the city's craft heritage: a small, precise intervention that changes the texture of the whole dish, applied so quietly that you might not notice it the first time. Once you do, it becomes the thing you think about afterward.

## The Artisan Context

Kanazawa has been a centre of craft production since the Edo period — Kenroku-en garden, Kutani ceramics, Wajima lacquerware, Kaga silk dyeing, and gold leaf production that currently supplies ninety-nine percent of Japan's domestic total. The food culture sits inside this context, and the best restaurants in the city understand themselves as part of the same tradition: precision craft applied to seasonal ingredients, presented in locally made ceramics, in rooms that would be unremarkable in the seventeenth century except for the view.

The *ryotei* restaurants around Higashi Chaya — the geisha district, one of the best-preserved in Japan — are where this tradition reaches its most formal expression. Several have been operating for over a century. Dinner at one of them is not inexpensive, but Kanazawa has enough mid-range options working within the same tradition that the city's food culture is accessible at most budgets. The lunch counter at Omicho market, a bowl of crab donburi with the morning's catch, costs considerably less than the kaiseki dinner and communicates the same essential thing about the place.

## A Note on Timing

The Hokuriku Shinkansen extension reached Kanazawa in 2015, cutting the journey from Tokyo to two and a half hours. Tourist footfall increased substantially. What the growth has not changed is the essential character of the food culture — a city that spent four centuries in relative isolation developing its own distinct approach to craft and cuisine, and that is not particularly interested in revising it for a wider audience.

Come in winter for the crab. Come in autumn for *nodoguro* at the peak of its fat content. Come in spring when the mountain vegetables — *takenoko* bamboo shoots, *warabi* bracken fern — begin to appear in the market stalls alongside the last of the season's seafood. The city rewards specificity of timing in the same way that all food cultures do, and more than most.

---

Kanazawa is a small city by Japanese standards — around 460,000 people — and it has no interest in competing with Tokyo or Osaka on their own terms. What it offers instead is a food culture that has had four centuries to become entirely itself, in a city that has had the same amount of time to build the ceramics and lacquerware to serve it in. That is a specific and unusual kind of excellence, and it is worth the journey.
