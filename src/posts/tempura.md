Tempura arrived in Japan via Portuguese missionaries in the sixteenth century — the word itself derives from *tempora*, the Latin term for the Ember Days when Catholics abstained from meat and fried vegetables instead. The Japanese took the technique, refined it over three centuries, and produced something that bears very little resemblance to the original. What Portuguese missionaries fried was a thick batter that added flavour and texture. What Japanese chefs refined toward was the opposite: a batter so light and so hot that it functions primarily as a vehicle for steam, cooking the ingredient inside while leaving almost nothing behind.

The goal of good tempura is not the coating. The goal is to make you forget the coating is there. That takes considerably more skill than it sounds, and the gap between acceptable tempura and exceptional tempura is one of the wider gaps in Japanese cooking.

## The Batter

Tempura batter is made from three ingredients: low-gluten flour, ice-cold water, and egg. The mixing is deliberately incomplete — lumps are left in, the batter is kept at near-freezing temperature, and the whole process takes about thirty seconds. Gluten development, which makes bread elastic and pasta toothsome, makes tempura dense and heavy. The objective is to prevent it entirely.

The fat used for frying is traditionally a mixture of sesame oil and vegetable oil, which produces the particular fragrance that fills a tempura counter — nutty and clean, not heavy. Temperature is everything: too low and the batter absorbs oil and turns greasy; too high and the outside browns before the inside cooks. The correct range is around 170 to 180 degrees Celsius for most ingredients, adjusted upward for dense vegetables and downward for delicate fish.

A piece of well-fried tempura, removed from the oil at the correct moment, is almost dry to the touch. The coating shatters lightly under the teeth and then is simply gone, leaving only the flavour of the ingredient.

## What Gets Fried

Prawns (*ebi*) are the standard benchmark — a large kuruma prawn, split lengthwise to prevent curling, fried for about ninety seconds, and eaten immediately. The flesh should be just set, yielding but not rubbery, with the sweetness of fresh seafood amplified by the brief heat.

*Kakiage* is the collective noun for a different approach: small pieces of multiple ingredients — shrimp, squid, vegetables — combined into a loose, lacy fritter rather than individually coated. The structure is deliberately irregular, producing a varied texture across each bite. Eaten over a bowl of rice (*kakiage don*) with a dashi-based sauce and grated daikon, it is one of the great practical lunch dishes of Tokyo.

Seasonal vegetables are where the serious tempura chef demonstrates their judgement. Young lotus root in summer; shiso leaf fried for eight seconds with batter on one side only; sweet potato from October; *maitake* mushroom with its frilled edges, which create an exceptionally delicate surface in the oil. The selection at a counter restaurant changes weekly according to what the season is actually producing rather than what the menu has scheduled.

## Counter Eating

The right way to eat serious tempura is at a counter in front of the chef, who fries each piece individually and serves it directly to your plate as it comes out of the oil. This is not a format that accommodates pacing yourself. Each piece arrives at the moment of maximum perfection and requires immediate attention. Letting it sit — even for two minutes — allows the batter to steam from the inside and lose its texture.

The dipping sauce (*tentsuyu*) is a warm dashi broth seasoned with mirin and soy, served with finely grated daikon and ginger. The alternative is flaked sea salt, which some chefs prefer for delicate ingredients where the tentsuyu would overwhelm the flavour. The correct choice depends on what you are eating and what the chef recommends, and it is worth asking.

## Where to Eat It

Tokyo is the centre of tempura culture. The most formal expressions are in Ginza and Nihonbashi, where restaurants like **Tsunahachi** (operating since 1923), **Mikawa**, and **Kondo** — whose vegetable tempura is considered among the finest in the country — serve multi-course counter meals starting at around 10,000 yen per person.

At the other end of the register: **Tendon Tenya**, a nationwide chain that serves a bowl of rice topped with prawn and vegetable tempura for around 600 yen, is a legitimate and respectable option for a weekday lunch. The gap between Tenya and Kondo is enormous — in price, in technique, in the kind of attention being paid — but Tenya is not a poor substitute. It is a different thing, and it is very good at being that different thing.

---

There is a category of Japanese food that looks simple on first encounter and reveals its difficulty slowly — you eat it three times before you notice what you were missing, and by the fourth time you have started to understand what is actually hard about it. Tempura is one of those. The batter disappears. What you are left with is a prawn, or a sweet potato, or a shiso leaf. They taste more like themselves than they did before they were fried. Explaining exactly why is the work of a lifetime at the counter.
