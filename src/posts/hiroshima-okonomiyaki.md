*Okonomiyaki* translates loosely as "grilled as you like it" — a savoury pancake of batter, vegetables, and protein cooked on a flat iron griddle. In Osaka, the ingredients are mixed together before cooking. In Hiroshima, they are layered: batter, cabbage, bean sprouts, meat, and noodles, each stratum distinct, the whole thing assembled with the precision of a construction project. The Hiroshima version takes longer to cook, is harder to make well, and produces a more complex result.

The dish is inseparable from the city's history. In August 1945, the food supply of a destroyed city largely reduced to *issen-yoki* — a simple pancake made from wheat flour and water, cooked on iron plates in the rubble. The okonomiyaki that exists today grew directly from that wartime food, building out from the basic batter as ingredients became available. By the 1950s, the shops that would define the city's food identity were already established.

## The Noodle Question

The detail that most distinguishes Hiroshima okonomiyaki from its Osaka counterpart is the noodles. The Hiroshima version includes a layer of yakisoba or udon, cooked separately on the griddle and then incorporated into the layered structure. The noodles soften slightly from the moisture of the other ingredients but retain some texture, providing a starch element that makes the dish genuinely filling.

The layering sequence matters. A skilled cook begins with a thin crepe of batter, adds shredded cabbage (a large amount — it cooks down considerably) and bean sprouts, then a choice of pork belly, squid, or prawn, then the noodles, then another thin layer of batter to seal the top, before flipping the entire structure with a wide spatula in a single, decisive motion. An egg is cracked onto the griddle and the pancake placed on top of it, incorporating the egg into the base. The whole process takes about ten minutes and requires total attention.

## Okonomimura

The most famous destination for Hiroshima okonomiyaki is Okonomimura — the "okonomiyaki village" — a three-storey building in central Hiroshima that houses around twenty shops. Each shop consists of a counter with four to eight seats arranged around a large iron griddle, and a single cook who works the griddle and serves the customers.

The building has been operating since 1945, rebuilt and expanded multiple times. The shops vary in style — some use pork, some specialise in seafood variations, some offer cheese or kimchi additions — but the fundamental dish is consistent. Prices are modest: a standard pork and noodle okonomiyaki runs to around 1,000 yen.

The best approach is to walk the building before sitting down, watching the cooks work. The one who is moving with the most economy — few wasted motions, griddle always in use, customers never waiting — tends to be the one whose food you want.

## The Rest of the City

Hiroshima's okonomiyaki culture extends well beyond Okonomimura. The city has hundreds of dedicated shops, and the standard of the average neighbourhood place is high. The area around Hondori shopping arcade and the streets south of Hiroshima Castle have good independent options; *Micchan*, one of the oldest shops in the city, operates from a small storefront near Shintenchi and has been doing so since the 1950s.

In autumn and winter, many shops serve a version with extra cheese and a drizzle of Japanese mustard sauce — both additions that the purists ignore and everyone else eats happily.

## How to Eat It

Okonomiyaki is eaten with a small flat spatula — the *hera* — which you use to cut sections from the pancake while it sits on its own small iron plate, keeping it warm. The sauce (a thick, sweet-savoury variant of Worcestershire) is applied generously, as is *kewpie* mayonnaise and, optionally, powdered aonori (dried seaweed) and katsuobushi (bonito flakes, which tremble in the heat).

You eat it directly from the iron plate. You eat it hot, because cold okonomiyaki is a different and considerably lesser thing. You will burn your mouth on the first bite, and you will do it again on the second.

---

Hiroshima is a city that has built something remarkable on top of an absence that should have made reconstruction impossible. The okonomiyaki is part of that story — food that evolved from almost nothing into something that defines a place, that people travel specifically to eat, that carries in its layered construction something of the patience and deliberateness the rebuilding required. That may be too much to put on a pancake. But in Hiroshima, it does not feel like it.
