Nagoya sits at the geographic and economic centre of Japan — two hours from Tokyo by Shinkansen, forty minutes from Osaka — but occupies an eccentric position in the national consciousness. Tokyo considers it provincial. Osaka considers it joyless. Nagoyans, characteristically unbothered by either assessment, have spent the better part of a century building one of the most distinctive regional food cultures in the country.

The term *Nagoya-meshi* — Nagoya food — was coined by the city's restaurant industry in the early 2000s, a deliberate act of marketing designed to draw tourists off the Shinkansen and into the restaurants. It succeeded partly because there was genuine substance behind the label: a cuisine built around one defining ingredient, deployed across half a dozen dishes in ways that no other Japanese city had thought to attempt.

That ingredient is *Hatcho miso*, a deeply fermented soybean paste produced in the nearby town of Okazaki that has been ageing in cedar barrels since the Edo period. Where most Japanese miso ferments for weeks or months, Hatcho miso ferments for a minimum of two summers — sometimes three — under weights of stacked river stone. The result is almost black, intensely savoury, and faintly bitter, with a complexity that paler misos cannot match.

## Miso Katsu

The most famous application is *miso katsu*: a pork cutlet — panko-crumbed, fried in the standard way — served under a ladle of hot Hatcho miso sauce rather than the Worcestershire-based tonkatsu sauce used everywhere else in Japan. The result is considerably more assertive and interesting than the original. What would otherwise be a pleasant but predictable dish becomes almost aggressive in its depth. Yabaton, the chain most associated with miso katsu, has been serving it since 1947 and operates over forty branches in the city.

*Miso nikomi udon* deploys the same logic toward noodles: udon simmered in a Hatcho miso broth in an earthenware pot with chicken, fish cake, and a raw egg cracked in at the end. The noodles are deliberately harder than the udon norm — firmer, chewier — because they will continue to soften in the residual heat of the pot after it leaves the kitchen.

## Hitsumabushi

Nagoya's other defining dish sits at the opposite end of the flavour register. *Hitsumabushi* is built around *unagi* — freshwater eel — an ingredient revered across Japan but rarely treated as distinctively as it is here.

Elsewhere, grilled eel arrives in a lacquered box over rice, to be eaten simply. In Nagoya, the eel is grilled over charcoal, glazed with a somewhat lighter tare than the national standard, chopped finely, and served over a wooden tub of rice. The custom is to eat it in three successive passes: first, plain from the tub, tasting the eel and rice together; second, with condiments stirred in at the table — wasabi, fine-cut nori, spring onion; third, by pouring a ladle of hot dashi broth over the remaining rice, converting the bowl into a *chazuke*. Each pass is a different dish from the same ingredients.

The restaurant most associated with the dish is **Atsuta Horaiken**, which has been operating near the Atsuta Shrine since 1873. Queues begin forming before it opens.

## Morning Service

A detail that separates Nagoya from every other Japanese city: its *morning service* coffee shop culture. At cafés around the city, ordering a coffee before 11am earns you a free breakfast — thick toast with a boiled egg, sometimes a small salad, occasionally miso soup — without it appearing on the menu or the bill. You pay for the coffee. The food is simply included.

This practice emerged from Nagoya's *kissaten* culture in the postwar decades and has never spread meaningfully to the rest of Japan. Locals eat breakfast in coffee shops as a matter of routine. The cafés fill by 8am and the morning service ends at eleven, after which they become regular coffee shops.

## Completing the Canon

The remaining Nagoya-meshi dishes round out the picture. *Tebasaki* chicken wings, fried twice to achieve a glassy caramelised skin and seasoned with soy, sugar, and black pepper, originating at the Furaibo chain in the 1960s. *Kishimen*, the city's flat ribbon noodles, served in a clear broth with dried bonito shavings and fish cake. *Ogura toast* — thick white bread spread with sweet red bean paste and butter — a combination invented at a Nagoya café in the 1920s and still a standard breakfast order throughout the region.

None of these dishes has much in common with each other, and that is partly the point. Nagoya-meshi is not a coherent culinary philosophy — it is a collection of local obsessions that happened to accumulate in the same city over the same century.

---

Nagoya's food culture reflects a city that has historically cared less about what Tokyo thinks than about doing things its own way. The Shinkansen has always made it easy to pass through without stopping. That, at the table, turns out to have been a significant mistake.
