The standard ryokan dinner begins around six in the evening and ends around nine. During those three hours, somewhere between eight and fourteen courses will arrive in the room — not at a dining table, but at a low table on a tatami floor, served by a woman in a kimono who will remember your name, your food preferences from the questionnaire you filled out at check-in, and that you asked for an extra pot of tea at breakfast.

The format is a variant of *kaiseki ryori*, the multi-course cuisine that developed in Kyoto's high culture and spread from there into every serious dining context in Japan. At a city kaiseki restaurant, the courses are calibrated against the chef's vision, the season, and the particular ceramics chosen to carry them. At a ryokan, the same logic applies, but with an additional layer: the meal is inseparable from the room, the *onsen* bath you have just taken, the sound of the river or the garden visible through the open screen door. The food and the setting are a single experience.

## The Structure

A full ryokan kaiseki proceeds in a fairly consistent order, though chefs vary the sequence according to their own logic and the demands of the specific menu.

*Sakizuke* — the opening course, a single small item that signals the season and sets the flavour register. It might be a piece of fresh tofu with a light dashi, or a small bite of marinated fish, or a single seasonal vegetable prepared with attention enough that it demands to be noticed.

*Hassun* — a tray with several small dishes arranged around a seasonal centrepiece, often a natural object — a branch, a stone, a piece of lacquerware that references the season. The hassun is the course that most directly communicates what the chef is thinking about this particular time of year.

*Mukōzuke* — the sashimi course. Three or five types of raw fish, served cold, on ceramics chosen to complement the colour of the fish. At a coastal ryokan, the fish will have arrived that morning. At an inland ryokan in Kyoto, the fish will have arrived by the morning express and will have been selected with corresponding care.

*Takiawase* — vegetables, tofu, or fish simmered separately and served in the same dish, each element retaining its individual character. The broth is typically light — a gentle dashi that connects the components without overwhelming any of them.

*Yakimono* — the grilled course. Usually fish: a small whole fish, a fillet, or a piece of shellfish, grilled with salt and perhaps a brush of mirin. The skin should be crisp and the flesh just set at the bone.

Rice, miso soup, and pickles arrive at the end, signalling that the meal is complete. Some ryokan serve a small dessert; others consider the pickles their own form of conclusion.

## The Seasonal Imperative

The word that appears most often in conversations about ryokan dining is *shun* — the peak season of an ingredient, the brief window when it is at its maximum quality. Japanese culinary culture treats shun with a seriousness that other food cultures apply more selectively, and nowhere is this more apparent than at the ryokan dinner table.

In March: *takenoko* (bamboo shoots), harvested from the mountain slopes surrounding Kyoto at a particular stage of growth when they are tender enough to eat raw, sliced, with nothing added. In June: *ayu* (sweetfish) from the clear rivers of Gifu and Shiga, grilled whole, eaten by starting at the head. In October: *matsutake* mushrooms, the most expensive ingredient in Japan's domestic seasonal calendar, served in a clear soup or grilled with a brush of soy. In December: *kani* (crab), which restructures the menus of every coastal ryokan in the country for three months.

Missing these windows is not a disaster. Visiting in the wrong month for your most-wanted ingredient and finding something else of equal quality in its place is also a valid form of education.

## What to Do During the Three Hours

The pace of a ryokan kaiseki is slow by design. Each course arrives when the kitchen is ready for it, which is not necessarily when you are ready for it. The correct approach is to surrender the evening's schedule entirely: put the phone away, refill the sake cup, and talk to the person across the low table.

The *nakai* — the attendant assigned to your room — will return periodically to pour drinks, clear dishes, and respond to requests. It is worth asking about the food: where the fish came from, what the ceramic was made in, why a particular vegetable was chosen for this course. The answers are not always forthcoming from reticent attendants, but when they are, they add a dimension that transforms the meal from a sequence of very good things into a comprehensible argument about a particular place at a particular moment in the year.

## Choosing a Ryokan

The range of quality in ryokan dining is wide. At the top end — the Relais & Châteaux properties of Kyoto, the celebrated onsen ryokan of Hakone and Kinosaki Onsen — the kitchen employs formally trained chefs and the meal is a serious event. At the other end, the food is functional and the ceremony minimal.

A reliable indicator: the quality of the dashi in the soup served at breakfast. Good dashi — made from real kombu and real katsuobushi, with mineral depth and clarity — indicates a kitchen that is paying attention to fundamentals. Poor dashi, thin and over-salted, indicates a kitchen that is cutting corners in ways that will be visible throughout the dinner.

---

The ryokan dinner asks a specific thing of its guests: to be in the room, with the food, for as long as the food takes. This is not a demand that modern life prepares people well for. Those who accept it — who surrender three hours to a sequence of small courses that are not trying to impress, only to demonstrate that they know what month it is — tend to find that it is among the better uses of an evening they have found in Japan.
