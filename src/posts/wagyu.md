The word *wagyu* means, simply, Japanese cattle. It is not a single breed — it refers to four breeds, the most important of which is Japanese Black (*kuroge washu*), which accounts for around ninety percent of Japanese beef production. What all four share is a genetic predisposition toward intramuscular fat deposition — the marbling that makes wagyu distinctive and that, at its extreme, produces beef in which the fat and lean are so thoroughly interlaced that the meat appears almost white.

Outside Japan, wagyu has become a premium marketing category applied to cattle of varying breeds and provenance, some with tenuous genetic connections to the original Japanese lines. Inside Japan, it is a more specific conversation: about which region raises the best cattle, under what conditions, eating what, for how long, and to what grade. The conversation is ongoing and occasionally heated.

## The Grading System

Japanese wagyu is graded by the Japan Meat Grading Association on a scale from 1 to 5 for yield (the proportion of usable meat from the carcass) and A to C for quality. Quality is further assessed on four criteria: marbling, meat colour, fat colour, and firmness and texture. The headline number that restaurants and butchers cite is typically the marbling score, assessed on a fifteen-point *BMS* (Beef Marbling Standard) scale.

A5 with BMS 10 or above is the top category — the beef that appears in glass cases in Tokyo department stores with individual price tags and that tourists photograph before buying. The texture is unlike any other beef: the fat melts at body temperature, the meat has a delicacy that requires minimal cooking, and the flavour is intensely savoury in a way that relates to the high glutamate content of well-marbled Japanese beef.

Whether this is the most pleasurable beef to eat is a separate question from whether it is the most prestigious, and serious eaters in Japan are not entirely agreed on the answer.

## The Regional Argument

Japan's prefectures take their beef seriously in the way that French regions take their wine seriously, and with comparable levels of provincial pride.

**Kobe beef** is the most internationally famous, produced from Tajima-strain Japanese Black cattle raised in Hyogo Prefecture under specific conditions — they must be born, raised, and slaughtered in the prefecture — and achieving a BMS of 6 or above. The fame is partly deserved (Kobe beef is exceptional) and partly a product of being the first Japanese beef that reached international markets in any volume.

**Matsusaka beef**, from Mie Prefecture, is the preference of many Japanese connoisseurs. The cattle here are all female, never having calved, and are raised for an unusually long period — thirty to forty months — on a diet that includes beer in the final feeding stage, a practice whose effect on flavour is debated but whose marketing value is clear. The fat quality is considered exceptional even by wagyu standards.

**Omi beef**, from Shiga Prefecture, is the oldest continuously documented Japanese beef brand, with records going back to the Edo period when it was presented to the shogun as tribute. It is less internationally known than Kobe and makes no particular effort to change this, which is itself a kind of confidence.

**Miyazaki beef** from Kyushu has won the national wagyu competition — held every five years — three consecutive times, a fact the prefecture publicises with understandable energy.

## How to Eat It

The cooking methods suited to high-grade wagyu are minimal. The fat melts at around 25 degrees Celsius, which means the beef begins to change texture simply from being handled. High heat for a short time — a few seconds on each side on a hot iron plate (*teppanyaki*), or briefly dipped in a light broth (*shabu-shabu*) — is sufficient.

*Sukiyaki* — wagyu cooked at the table in a shallow iron pan with soy sauce, mirin, and sugar — is the traditional celebration format. Each piece of beef is dipped in a raw egg after cooking, which tempers the sweetness of the cooking liquid and adds a richness that contrasts with the meat. It is the format associated with formal occasions: company dinners, new year gatherings, expensive dates where the bill is not discussed in advance.

*Yakiniku* — grilled at the table over charcoal or gas, dipped in a sauce of soy, sesame, and citrus — democratises the ingredient slightly. The best yakiniku restaurants in Tokyo's Nishiazabu neighbourhood serve cuts that would not look out of place at a kaiseki dinner, priced accordingly.

## A Word on Restraint

A common mistake with high-grade wagyu is eating too much of it. Three or four slices of A5 ribeye is the correct quantity — enough to understand what you are tasting, not so much that the richness becomes oppressive. The fat, at this intensity, has a physical effect on the palate that makes eating more than a modest portion uncomfortable.

The Japanese understanding of this is embedded in the way wagyu is served: kaiseki courses might include one or two small pieces as a single course; a teppanyaki set will allocate a specific quantity per person and stop there. The restraint is not about cost, or not only about that. It is an acknowledgement that the ingredient makes its point quickly and does not benefit from repetition.

---

Wagyu's reputation outside Japan has been complicated by the ease with which the word can be used without meaningful precision. Inside Japan, the question is never whether the beef is wagyu but which animal, from where, eating what, graded to what standard, and prepared by whose hands. Those distinctions take a lifetime to fully appreciate, and they are worth beginning.
