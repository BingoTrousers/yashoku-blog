Japan has three canonical ramen styles that most people outside Japan know about: Tokyo's soy-based shoyu, Kyushu's tonkotsu, and Sapporo's miso. The first two have exported well — shoyu ramen is restrained and elegant, tonkotsu is rich and aggressive and easy to love on first contact. Miso ramen is harder to explain. It is the most complex of the three, the most adapted to its landscape, and arguably the most rewarding.

Hokkaido is Japan's northernmost major island — a place of vast agricultural plains, heavy snowfall, and winters that make Tokyo's feel Mediterranean. The food that developed here reflects those conditions: rich dairy from cattle farms, king crab from the frigid seas, corn so sweet it requires no seasoning, and a ramen style built to provide maximum warmth from a single bowl.

## What Makes It Different

The defining characteristic of Sapporo miso ramen is fat. Not just the miso — a fermented soybean paste that adds depth and umami — but the lard or butter that is often worked into the broth, and the corn and bean sprouts that are stir-fried in a wok before being added to the bowl. The result is a broth with a slick, glossy surface and a richness that sits differently in the stomach than the lighter broths of the south.

The noodles are wavy and medium-thick, chosen to hold the broth rather than float in it. The toppings typically include *chashu* pork (braised belly, sliced and torched), bamboo shoots, spring onion, and often a knob of butter placed directly on the surface of the broth, where it melts slowly as you eat.

## Ramen Yokocho

Sapporo has its own ramen alley — *Ganso Ramen Yokocho*, in the Susukino district — which has been operating since 1951. Nineteen small shops open from late afternoon until the early hours of the morning, each with its own recipe and its own loyal following. The alley is narrow and steam-filled in winter, each shop spilling warmth and the smell of fermented miso into the freezing air outside.

The etiquette in Ramen Yokocho is uncomplicated: choose your shop by looking at the menu boards outside, sit down, order quickly, eat, and leave. The shops are small — most seat fewer than twelve people — and the turnover is fast. You are not expected to linger, which in winter is fine, because the instinct after eating is to walk back into the cold and feel the warmth radiating outward from your centre.

Among the original shops, **Aji no Karyu** and **Keyaki** are the most frequently cited by locals as benchmarks for the style. Both have been operating for decades and both have the kind of consistency that comes from doing one thing for a very long time.

## Beyond the Alley

Sapporo's ramen culture extends well beyond the famous alley. The *Sapporo Ramen Republic* on the tenth floor of the ESTA building near the train station gathers eight shops from around Hokkaido under one roof — useful for sampling regional variations without travelling the island. Hokkaido has its own regional sub-styles: Asahikawa's shoyu ramen is made with a double broth of pork and seafood and served with a thick layer of oil to retain heat; Hakodate's ramen is lighter and more delicate, closer to the clear broths of the mainland.

But Sapporo miso remains the reference point, and the Susukino district at night — lit up and crowded even in deep winter, with the snow piled on the street corners and the izakaya spilling music into the cold — is the right place to eat it.

## A Note on Timing

Ramen in Sapporo is a late-night food. The best shops do good trade at lunchtime, but their real hours are after 9pm, when the bars in Susukino are full and people want something warm and substantial before the walk home. In deep winter, this is also when the cold is at its most serious, and arriving at a small ramen counter after twenty minutes in minus-fifteen air gives the broth a quality it probably doesn't have in September.

---

There is a theory that the best food is always calibrated to its climate — that the richness of French butter sauces makes sense only against a grey Normandy winter, that Sichuan's numbing spice is inseparable from its humid summers. Sapporo miso ramen supports this theory. Eat it in July and it is very good. Eat it in February, after dark, with the snow still falling, and it is something else entirely.
