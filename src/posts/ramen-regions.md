Japan's ramen culture is regional in the way that wine culture is regional — the same fundamental concept expressed through local ingredients, local water, local taste preferences, and a century of accumulated local argument about what makes a bowl correct. The map of regional ramen styles does not align neatly with the map of prefectures; it is more granular than that, organised around cities and sub-cities and the particular decisions made by individual shop owners whose variations became, over time, local orthodoxy.

Five styles have developed sufficient coherence and national recognition to function as reference points. Understanding them is not the same as knowing Japanese ramen — the field is too large — but it is a useful frame for the many variations that sit between and around them.

## Tokyo: Shoyu

The original city ramen, and still arguably the reference point against which others are measured. Tokyo-style shoyu ramen is a clear amber broth — made from chicken and sometimes fish, seasoned with soy sauce — with thin, straight, slightly curly noodles. The toppings are the classic canon: *chashu* pork (belly, braised in soy and mirin), *menma* bamboo shoots, nori, spring onion, a marinated soft-boiled egg (*ajitsuke tamago*).

The broth is intended to be transparent enough to see the noodles at the bottom of the bowl. The flavour is savoury and clean without heaviness. It is the style that rewards the bowl it came from — the quality of the chicken, the care taken with the soy seasoning (*tare*) — more than it rewards theatrics.

**Taishoken**, in Higashi-Ikebukuro, is associated with the invention of *tsukemen* (dipping ramen) and has been serving a version of this style since 1961. **Fuunji** in Shinjuku expanded the Tokyo style in a different direction — a dense, fish-forward broth with a richness that surprised the local scene when it opened and has since acquired a large and loyal queue.

## Hakata: Tonkotsu

Fukuoka's contribution to the national conversation: a broth made by boiling pork bones — *ton* (pork) *kotsu* (bone) — at a rolling boil for many hours, which causes the collagen to emulsify into the liquid and turn the broth opaque white and intensely rich. The noodles are thin and straight, the thinnest of any major ramen style, able to cook in under a minute.

The Hakata format produces a bowl that is the opposite of Tokyo shoyu in almost every respect: cloudy, heavy, richly flavoured, with a smell that carries across the room. The toppings are minimal — chashu, spring onion, *beni shoga* (pickled red ginger), sesame seeds — because the broth requires no assistance.

The defining Hakata custom is *kaedama*: when your bowl is almost finished, you request an additional serving of noodles (*tama*) for a nominal charge, dropped into the remaining broth. You are not being offered more soup. You are being offered more noodles to finish the soup you already have.

## Sapporo: Miso

Hokkaido's cold climate shaped a ramen style built around warmth and richness in the way that Hakata's urban density shaped a style built around speed. Sapporo miso ramen combines a chicken or pork base with generous amounts of miso — sometimes a blend of types — and is finished with lard or butter, stir-fried vegetables, and often a piece of corn. The noodles are wavy and medium-thick.

The result is the most complex of the canonical styles, with a depth that builds across the bowl rather than announcing itself immediately. It is also the most caloric, which in Hokkaido in February is not a criticism.

## Kitakata: Shoyu with Pork Bone

Kitakata, a small city in Fukushima Prefecture, has the highest concentration of ramen shops per capita in Japan — more than one for every 500 residents — in a city of around 50,000 people. The local style is a shoyu broth made with pork bone and niboshi (dried sardines) as the base, with fat, wavy noodles that are among the widest in Japanese ramen.

Kitakata ramen is eaten for breakfast, a custom that persists from the city's history as a sake-producing town where morning eating around the brewery warehouses created an early-rising food culture. Showing up at a Kitakata shop before 9am and ordering ramen as a first meal of the day is not eccentric. It is the correct approach.

## Asahikawa: Double Broth

Asahikawa, in central Hokkaido, developed a style that straddles the Sapporo miso tradition and the Tokyo shoyu tradition: a double-stock broth made from both pork bones and seafood (usually dried seafood — scallop, shrimp, kelp), seasoned with soy rather than miso, and served with a thick layer of rendered lard floating on the surface to retain heat in the extreme cold.

The visual effect — an amber broth almost invisible beneath a disc of white fat — puts off the uninitiated. The fat is not decorative. It functions as insulation, keeping the broth at eating temperature while you make your way through the bowl, and it disperses through the broth as you eat, adding richness without dominating the flavour.

## A Working Approach

Eating your way through Japan's ramen styles is not a project that can be completed. New shops open, established shops close or evolve, regional substyles diverge and converge. The more useful goal is to eat any regional style in the region where it developed — Hakata tonkotsu in Fukuoka, Sapporo miso in Hokkaido — because the bowl calibrated to its local climate and local palate is a different thing from the same style reproduced elsewhere, however accurately.

---

The argument about which regional style is best has been running in Japan for the better part of a century and shows no sign of resolution. This is appropriate. The question is not meant to be answered — it is meant to be pursued, one bowl at a time, until you have covered enough of the map to understand how large it actually is.
