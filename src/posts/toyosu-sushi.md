The Toyosu Market opened in 2018, when the old Tsukiji inner market — Tokyo's famous wholesale fish trading hub, operating since 1935 — finally relocated to a purpose-built facility on a reclaimed island in Tokyo Bay. The move was controversial. Tsukiji's outer market still operates and still draws enormous crowds, but the wholesale auctions, the licensed brokers, the pre-dawn dealing in bluefin tuna — all of that moved to Toyosu.

The result is a market that is more ordered, more hygienic, and considerably less atmospheric than its predecessor. It is also, for anyone interested in where the fish that ends up on Tokyo's sushi counters actually comes from, one of the most extraordinary places in the world to spend an early morning.

## The Tuna Auction

Access to the tuna auction is restricted. A limited number of visitor spots are available through a pre-registration system on the Toyosu Market website, and demand consistently outstrips supply. If you are lucky enough to secure a place, you arrive before 5am at the designated visitor area and watch from an observation deck as licensed buyers move through the rows of frozen bluefin, each fish tagged with its origin — Pacific, Atlantic, Mediterranean — and its weight. The buyers are fast and inscrutable; they assess the tail cross-section with a flashlight, make notes, and move on.

The auction itself lasts about twenty minutes. What you witness is the moment at which the global tuna industry resolves into a price, and from that price — the most expensive individual fish regularly sells for millions of yen — the economics of Tokyo's sushi market descend in a line all the way to the counter in front of you.

## Breakfast Sushi

Whether or not you attend the auction, Toyosu's intermediate wholesale market opens to the general public at 9am, and the cluster of sushi shops in the Uogashi area within the market building serves some of the best breakfast sushi in the city. These are not tourist operations. They exist to feed the market workers — buyers, handlers, drivers — who have been working since before dawn and want to eat well quickly.

The format is *kaiten-zushi* (conveyor belt) or counter omakase, starting from around 600 yen per plate and rarely running past 3,000 yen for a full breakfast. The fish is exceptional — it came through the auction a few hours ago — and it is served at a temperature and in a state of freshness that most evening sushi restaurants cannot match.

The best counter in the market area, in the opinion of most people who visit regularly, does not have a sign in English and does not take reservations. You join the queue at 8.30am and wait.

## The Outer Tsukiji Market

After Toyosu, or as an alternative for those who don't make the early morning trip to the island, the Tsukiji Outer Market remains one of the best places in Tokyo to eat and shop. The 150-metre covered arcade has been a retail and eating district since the 1930s, and while the inner market is gone, the retail stalls — dried fish, pickles, kitchen tools, street food counters — continue.

The tamagoyaki (sweet rolled omelette) sold at the dedicated counter near the Shin-Ohashi entrance is worth seeking out specifically. The version here is thicker and more restrained in sweetness than most, cooked in a rectangular pan over a wood flame, and eaten hot from a stick. Several vendors make it to order, which produces a small, genuinely satisfying piece of street theatre alongside a very good omelette.

## What to Know

The tuna auction observation slots open for registration months in advance and fill quickly; check the Tokyo Metropolitan Central Wholesale Market website for current registration windows. Arrive at Toyosu Station (Yurikamome line) at least thirty minutes before your booked slot.

For the Outer Tsukiji Market, arrive before 9am to catch it at its best. The market quietens considerably by midday as vendors sell out and begin packing up. The area is walkable from Tsukiji Station (Hibiya line) or Higashi-Ginza Station (Asakusa line).

Bring cash. Bring an appetite.

---

Eating sushi close to its source — at 7am, after watching the fish that might be in front of you change hands at auction two hours earlier — changes something about how you understand the ingredient. The price and the care that attend a single bluefin tuna from the Pacific to a plate in Ginza are not abstract when you have watched a buyer move through the rows in the cold of a November morning, flashlight in hand. What arrives on the rice afterwards means more.
