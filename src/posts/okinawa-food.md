Okinawa is technically part of Japan — it has been since 1972, when the United States formally returned the archipelago after twenty-seven years of administration following the Second World War — but its food culture is not. Or rather, it is its own thing that has been absorbing influences for centuries: from Japan, from China, from the Ryukyu Kingdom that preceded both, and from a quarter-century of American military presence that introduced Spam to the island in quantities that fundamentally altered the local diet.

The result is a cuisine unlike anything else in the country — richer, porkier, less deferential to the delicacy that defines cooking on the mainland, and built around a set of ingredients and techniques that mainland Japanese people often find disorienting on first contact. The longevity statistics — Okinawa has historically had among the highest concentrations of centenarians anywhere in the world — have made the local diet a subject of international study. The food, eaten in the right places, is also simply very good.

## The Pork Axis

Okinawan cooking is built around pork in a way that the rest of Japan is not. Mainland Japanese cuisine uses pork — chashu in ramen, tonkatsu, kakuni — but treats it as one ingredient among many. In Okinawa, pork is the centre of gravity.

*Rafute* is the defining dish: pork belly braised slowly in a mixture of awamori (the local spirit, distilled from indica rice), soy sauce, and sugar until the fat has rendered almost completely and the meat falls apart at the lightest pressure. The braising liquid reduces to a syrup that glazes each piece. It is rich in a way that requires no accompaniment beyond a small cup of awamori and an acknowledgement that the mainland's ideas about restraint do not apply here.

*Tebichi* — pig's trotter, similarly braised — is a dish that rewards people who approach it without preconceptions. The gelatin content is extreme; the texture is somewhere between meat and aspic. Okinawans eat it enthusiastically and without ceremony.

*Mimigaa* — shredded pig's ear, served cold with a vinegar-sesame dressing — is the lightest entry point. The ear is mostly cartilage after cooking, with a snap under the tooth and very little fat. It is served as a side dish or bar snack and pairs naturally with the cold Orion beer that is the island's default drink.

## Champuru

*Champuru* is both a cooking method and a philosophy. The word means "something mixed" in the Okinawan language, and the dish is a stir-fry that combines tofu with whatever is available — vegetables, meat, egg — in proportions that vary by household and by season. The tofu used is a firmer, denser variety than the silken tofu of the mainland, able to hold its shape in the wok.

*Goya champuru* — with bitter melon (*goya*) as the primary vegetable — is the most celebrated version. The bitterness of the melon, the salt of the Spam or pork, the egg that binds everything loosely, and the firm tofu: it is a dish that should not add up to anything particular and turns out to be one of the more satisfying things you can eat in Japan. Whether this is because of the ingredients or because you ate it in the shade at a roadside restaurant with the ocean visible from the table is difficult to say.

## Soba That Isn't Soba

Okinawa soba is not soba. It contains no buckwheat — a fact that caused a brief regulatory dispute with the mainland in the 1970s over whether the name could be used. The noodles are made from wheat flour, round and firm, served in a pork and bonito broth with braised pork belly, fish cake, and pickled ginger. It is closer in structure to a light ramen than to any mainland soba, and it is eaten at all hours without particular reference to when it might be appropriate.

*Soki soba* — with spare rib (*soki*) replacing the standard pork belly — is the heartier version. The ribs are braised until the meat lifts cleanly from the bone and served in the bowl with the noodles. Order it at a roadside shop in the middle of the island, where the American military base occupies a significant portion of the surrounding land and the menu is in Japanese and English.

## Awamori

Okinawa's native spirit is *awamori*, distilled from Thai indica rice using a black koji mould rather than the yellow koji used in sake production. It is stronger than sake — typically 30 to 40 percent alcohol — and aged in clay pots, sometimes for decades. Old awamori (*kūsu*) has a smoky, complex quality that bears some resemblance to aged Scotch whisky without tasting like either sake or shochu.

The correct way to drink it is with water and ice (*mizu-wari*), which reduces the alcohol and opens up the aromatics. The incorrect way is to drink it straight at room temperature immediately upon sitting down, which will close the rest of the evening prematurely.

---

Okinawa's food culture has been shaped by two kinds of isolation: geographical distance from the Japanese mainland, and the particular experience of a small island that has been governed, in succession, by its own kingdom, by Japan, by the United States, and by Japan again. Each layer left something behind at the table. The result is an island that cooks unlike anywhere else in the country, and is rightly proud of it.
