Kagawa Prefecture occupies the northeastern corner of Shikoku, the smallest of Japan's four main islands, and has the most focused relationship with food of any Japanese region: it grows wheat, it makes udon, it eats a great deal of it, and it has been doing this for roughly a thousand years.

The statistics, which locals cite with modest pride, are unusual. Kagawa has roughly one million people and somewhere between six hundred and eight hundred udon shops, depending on the survey — one for approximately every 1,500 residents. Kagawa residents eat udon an average of five times a week. The regional variant, *Sanuki udon*, is formally recognised as a distinct style. The prefecture was briefly renamed, in a tourist promotion, *Udon Prefecture*. The campaign worked well enough that the name has stuck in a semi-official capacity.

None of this is branding confection. Kagawa genuinely runs on udon, and once you have eaten a bowl at a good shop there, the udon everywhere else in Japan starts to look like an approximation.

## What Makes It Different

Sanuki udon is distinguished primarily by its texture. The noodles are thick — thicker than most udon you will encounter in Tokyo — with a particular tautness and elasticity, what the Japanese call *koshi*, that gives way slowly under the tooth rather than snapping. This quality comes from the local soft wheat flour — *Sanuki Dream* is one of the preferred varieties — the high mineral content of Kagawa's water, and hand-kneading techniques that the best shops preserve even at high volume.

The broth is made from local *iriko* — small dried anchovies fished from the Seto Inland Sea — rather than the katsuobushi-dominant stocks more common on the mainland. The result is lighter and saltier, with an emphatic sea flavour, calibrated to the noodle's robustness rather than competing with it.

## The Styles

*Kamaage* udon arrives in the cooking pot itself, undrained, the noodles still in their hot cooking water, with a small dipping bowl of concentrated tsuyu on the side. It is the purest expression of the noodle — no other flavour, just wheat and hot water, dipped briefly and eaten quickly before it cools.

*Kama-tama* is a Kagawa original: the noodles are taken straight from the cooking pot and placed in a bowl with a raw egg, which is stirred in and coats them in a warm, silky glaze. Soy sauce is added at the table. It is one of the most satisfying things you can eat for three hundred yen.

*Bukkake* udon comes cold or warm with a small amount of concentrated dashi poured directly over the noodles, rather than the noodles sitting submerged in a full broth. Condiments — grated daikon, spring onion, a soft-cooked egg, sometimes a wedge of lemon — are added at the table according to preference.

## The Pilgrimage

Kagawa has two famous pilgrimage circuits. The first is the 88 temples of the Shikoku Henro, walked by Buddhist devotees in white clothing over forty to sixty days. The udon pilgrimage is shorter and less spiritually demanding.

A *udon taxi* — actual taxis whose drivers have curated multi-shop routes — operates in and around Takamatsu, the prefectural capital. Drivers plan routes of four to six shops, covering both the western and eastern parts of the prefecture where the most celebrated shops cluster, and take visitors to places that would be genuinely difficult to reach by public transport. The service is popular enough that it needs to be booked in advance.

The shops themselves vary from simple family operations — a room in someone's house, two tables, a queue forming before they open — to larger establishments that nonetheless operate on self-service logic: collect your bowl, add condiments from a communal counter, pay by noodle quantity on the way out. The benchmark shops include **Udon Baka Ichidai** in Takamatsu, **Yamada-ya** in the hills above the city, and **Nakamura** in Utazu, each with a particular approach to the noodle that makes comparison worthwhile.

## How the Shops Are Organised

Many of the best Kagawa udon shops are categorised as *serufu-sābisu* — self-service — which can disorient visitors used to table service. You join the queue, choose your noodle temperature and quantity from a small menu board, receive your bowl from the cook, collect toppings from a bar along the counter, and pay at the end. The whole process takes about three minutes. The efficiency is not unfriendly; it simply reflects how udon functions here — as a staple, not a destination.

Some of the most-discussed shops keep irregular hours and close once their daily supply of noodles is exhausted, which can happen by early afternoon. Arriving at 10am to find a note on the door — *udon ga nakunatta*, the udon is gone — is a recognised hazard of the pilgrimage.

## When to Go

Udon is a breakfast food in Kagawa in a way it is nowhere else in Japan. The best shops open by 7am or 8am and their earliest customers are elderly locals who have been making this particular journey for decades. Going early, standing at a counter with a bowl of kama-tama and a pot of tea for less than the price of a coffee elsewhere in Japan, is the part of the pilgrimage that the guidebooks do not quite capture.

---

Kagawa is a small prefecture with modest conventional tourist infrastructure. The udon is the reason to go, and it turns out to be enough. The Japanese understand this intuitively. The international tourist industry is still, slowly, catching up.
