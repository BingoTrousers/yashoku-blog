In most Japanese cities, the *yatai* — the portable outdoor food stall — is a relic. Post-war urban policy, hygiene regulations, and the general tidying impulse of modern civic planning eliminated them from Tokyo, Osaka, and Kyoto over the course of several decades. Fukuoka, on the northern tip of Kyushu, decided not to bother.

On any given evening from spring through autumn, and on most winter nights too, roughly a hundred yatai set up along the banks of the Naka River, in the streets of Tenjin, and under the covered alley of Nakasu. They are small — most seat between six and ten people — and they are run by a single person, or occasionally a couple, who cook everything from behind a narrow counter while their customers sit close enough to feel the heat.

## What They Serve

The menu at a yatai depends entirely on the person running it. Some specialise in *hakata ramen* — the tonkotsu style native to Fukuoka, with a broth so rich it coats the back of a spoon and noodles thin enough to cook in under a minute. Others do yakitori, threading chicken thighs and skin and cartilage onto skewers and grilling them over charcoal that glows orange in the dark. Some serve oden — a winter hot pot of fish cakes, daikon, and tofu simmered in a dashi broth — or *mentaiko*, the spicy cod roe that Fukuoka treats as a birthright.

The city's signature yatai dish, if one can be said to exist, is *hakata ramen* eaten at the counter at 11pm, with extra *kaedama* — a second serving of noodles dropped into your remaining broth for almost nothing. It is one of the great cheap pleasures of Japanese food culture, and it tastes better outside, under a canvas awning, with a cold beer.

## The Protocol

Yatai have their own etiquette, which is relaxed but real. You take a seat at whatever stall catches your eye — there is no queuing system, you simply sit down if there is room. You order quickly, because the cook is managing everything alone and the menu is short by design. You stay for a reasonable time and then leave; the stall has a capacity of eight people and a queue of people standing behind the plastic curtain who would like your seat.

Most yatai owners are happy to help with the menu if your Japanese is limited, and a surprising number speak some English — Fukuoka is an international city with strong ties to Korea and China, and its yatai culture has become one of its most-visited attractions.

## Where to Go

The three main yatai clusters are Nakasu, Tenjin, and Watanabe-dori. Nakasu, on a narrow strip of land between two rivers, has the highest concentration and the most atmospheric setting — lanterns reflect in the water and the city hums around you. Tenjin is slightly more relaxed and perhaps better for first-timers. Watanabe-dori, a few minutes' walk from the main action, tends to attract more locals.

The stalls open around 6pm and close when they run out of food or customers, which is usually sometime after midnight. The sweet spot is 8pm to 10pm, when the rush of early diners has passed and the cooks are in a good mood.

## Why It Matters

What makes Fukuoka's yatai culture worth travelling for is not any individual dish — the ramen is good, but you can eat very good ramen in Tokyo. What matters is the format: the enforced proximity to strangers, the single cook who is doing everything, the fact that the stall has been set up and will be broken down in a single evening. It is impermanent food in a way that almost nothing else in Japan is, and that impermanence gives it a particular quality of attention.

You eat it knowing it won't be there tomorrow in quite the same way.

---

Fukuoka has been quietly building a reputation as Japan's most liveable city for several years now — cheaper than Tokyo, warmer than Sapporo, more open than Kyoto. The yatai are a large part of why. They give the city its texture after dark, and they are the kind of thing that, once you have experienced it, makes you understand what most modern cities have given up.
