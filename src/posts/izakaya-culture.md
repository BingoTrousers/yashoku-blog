The word *izakaya* breaks into three parts: *i* (to stay), *sake* (alcohol), and *ya* (place). A place to stay and drink. The definition tells you almost nothing about what the experience is actually like, which is: loud, warm, slightly smoky, generously lit by pendant bulbs, and structured around a rhythm of small plates arriving at the table with no particular order or ceremony.

Japan has somewhere between 150,000 and 200,000 izakaya, depending on how loosely you define the category. They are found everywhere — basement rooms under office buildings, narrow slots between convenience stores, converted houses in residential streets, purpose-built chains that seat three hundred people across two floors. The format is so embedded in Japanese social life that to describe it as a type of restaurant undersells it. It is more like a civic institution.

## How It Works

You sit down — at a table, a booth, or at the counter facing the kitchen — and someone brings you a small plate of something you didn't order. This is the *otoshi*, an obligatory appetiser that also serves as a cover charge; it will appear on the bill whether you eat it or not. It is usually something simple: a sliver of tofu, a few pickled vegetables, edamame. It signals that the evening has begun.

From there, you order in rounds. The menu at most izakaya is extensive and built around sharing — each plate is smaller than a main course and designed to be passed around the table. You order four dishes, they arrive, you eat, you order four more. The rhythm continues until you are full, at which point you order more beer and keep talking.

## What to Eat

The core izakaya repertoire is built around a handful of dishes that have been refined over decades of iteration.

*Karaage* — Japanese fried chicken, marinated in soy and ginger and fried to a crisp — is the baseline by which an izakaya is judged. It should arrive hot, with a wedge of lemon and a small dish of kewpie mayonnaise. If the karaage is wrong — if it is soggy, or dry, or under-seasoned — it tells you something unflattering about the kitchen.

*Yakitori* arrives on skewers, two or three pieces per stick, grilled over charcoal. A proper yakitori counter is its own art form, with a chef who tends a bed of *binchotan* charcoal and rotates dozens of skewers in a precise sequence. In an izakaya setting it is less ceremonial but the good ones are still excellent — thigh meat (momo) glazed in tare sauce, skin (kawa) rendered until it blisters, and the neck meat (seseri) which rewards anyone who knows to order it.

*Dashimaki tamago* — a rolled omelette made with dashi — is one of those dishes that sounds simple and turns out to require considerable skill. The best versions are barely set, still yielding in the centre, tasting more of the sea than of egg. *Agedashi tofu* (silken tofu in a light dashi broth, just barely fried) and *hiyayakko* (cold tofu with ginger and scallion) offer the kitchen a chance to show what it does with subtler ingredients.

## What to Drink

Beer is the opening move — almost always a draft lager, ordered in a large glass, consumed quickly. After that, the classic izakaya progression moves to *highballs* (Japanese whisky and soda, served very cold and very fizzy), then to *sake* if the evening calls for it, then to *shochu* if it has run long.

The shochu options at a good izakaya can be bewildering — made from rice, barley, sweet potato, or buckwheat, served over ice or diluted with hot water. The correct choice depends on what you are eating and how long you plan to stay. Ask the staff. They will have an opinion.

## Finding the Right One

The distinction that matters is between *akachochin* izakaya and chain izakaya. The *akachochin* (red lantern) style — small, independent, run by someone who has been doing this for twenty years and has very strong views about the correct way to prepare certain dishes — is what most people mean when they talk about izakaya as a cultural experience. The chains (Torikizoku, Watami, Shidax) are consistent and cheap and fine, but they are to the real thing what a chain hotel is to a ryokan.

Look for the red lantern outside. Look for a hand-written menu on the wall. Look for somewhere that is completely full at 8pm on a Tuesday. Those are the signs.

---

The best evenings at an izakaya have no particular trajectory. They begin with intention — you were going to have two beers and go home — and they end somewhere else entirely, three hours later, having eaten too much and talked too honestly. That is what the format is designed to produce, and it is very good at it.
