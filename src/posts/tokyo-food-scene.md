There is a statistic that gets repeated so often it has almost lost its power to surprise: Tokyo has more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city on earth. More than Paris. More than New York. More than London and Hong Kong combined. The number fluctuates year to year, but it has been sitting somewhere above 200 for more than a decade.

The statistic is true, and it is also slightly misleading, because it implies that Tokyo's food culture is defined by its elite restaurants. It is not. What makes Tokyo extraordinary is the relentless quality across every level — the fact that a 700-yen bowl of ramen from a counter with six seats can be as technically accomplished as anything served in a room with white tablecloths.

## The Ramen Question

You cannot talk about Tokyo food without talking about ramen, and you cannot talk about Tokyo ramen without taking a position, because the city's ramen culture is deep enough to sustain genuine argument.

The local style — *Tokyo ramen* — uses a soy sauce-based broth (shoyu) that is lighter in body than the tonkotsu broths of Kyushu or the miso broths of Hokkaido, but layered with chicken, dashi, and often a dose of *tare* (concentrated seasoning) that gives it a savoury intensity far beyond its apparent simplicity. The noodles are typically wavy and medium-thickness, designed to carry the broth without overwhelming it.

**Fuunji** in Shinjuku is one of the best tsukemen (dipping ramen) shops in the city, with a rich, almost sauce-like dipping broth and thick noodles that hold up to it. Arrive before opening and join the queue. **Ichiran**, the solo-booth chain, is a reliable benchmark at any hour if you need something consistent and fast.

But ramen in Tokyo rewards exploration more than recommendation. Walk until you see a queue outside a small place that doesn't have an English sign, and eat there.

## Depachika: The Underground Food Halls

Beneath almost every major department store in Tokyo is a *depachika* — a basement food hall that functions simultaneously as a grocery, a prepared food market, a gift shop, and a temple to culinary obsession.

The depachika at Isetan in Shinjuku, Takashimaya in Nihonbashi, and Matsuya in Ginza are the most celebrated, but almost any department store basement will exceed your expectations. You will find: sushi made to order, wagyu beef boxed for gifts at prices that require you to sit down first, patisseries producing French pastry to a standard that would embarrass most Paris boulangeries, bento boxes assembled with evident pride, yakitori counters, soba shops, and more varieties of pickled vegetable than you thought existed.

The best time to visit is early evening, when some vendors begin marking down perishable items. The worst time is Sunday afternoon, when it is very nearly impossible to move.

## Tsukiji Outer Market

The inner market — the famous wholesale fish auction — moved to Toyosu in 2018. But the outer market, the ring of retail stalls and small restaurants surrounding the original site in Chuo ward, is still operating and still very much worth a morning.

Arrive by 7am. The sushi counters that line the market's main alleys are already busy by then, serving omakase breakfasts of whatever came in fresh that morning. Eight to twelve pieces, served across the counter directly from the chef's hand, eaten immediately. This is sushi without ceremony or theatre: the fish is exceptional, the rice is warm, and there is nowhere to sit except on a stool at a narrow counter with your elbows touching a stranger's.

Beyond sushi, the outer market is the place to find the tools of Japanese cooking — professional knives sold by the craftsmen who made them, copper pots, ceramic bowls, tamagoyaki pans. Even if you are not buying, it is worth walking.

## Yakitori Alley

Under the Yurakucho elevated train tracks, in the thin strip of space between concrete pillars, a cluster of yakitori restaurants has been operating since the 1950s. The trains rumble overhead every few minutes, the smoke from the charcoal grills hangs in the air, and the plastic chairs are arranged on a kind of collective terrace that spills out between the restaurants without clear boundaries.

This is one of Tokyo's great cheap-eating experiences. Order chicken in every form — thigh, skin, cartilage, liver, heart — alongside vegetables and simple salads, drink cold draft beer, and stay for two hours. Nobody will rush you.

The area around Shinjuku's Golden Gai and Memory Lane (Omoide Yokocho) offers similar experiences in smaller, more intimate spaces — eight-seat counters in buildings that seem structurally implausible, where the cook is close enough to talk to and usually happy to do so.

## Convenience Store Food (Seriously)

This sounds like a joke and is not. Japan's convenience store chains — 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson — operate at a standard of prepared food that has no equivalent elsewhere in the world. The onigiri (rice balls) are made fresh multiple times a day and are genuinely good. The sandwiches are soft, precisely made, and kept at a temperature that doesn't destroy the filling. The hot food counter serves karaage, nikuman (steamed pork buns), and croquettes that are reliably crisp and hot.

At 2am, after a long evening in a bar, a konbini (convenience store) is the best restaurant in Tokyo. The katsu sando — a fried pork cutlet sandwich on milk bread with Bulldog sauce — is worth eating at any hour.

## A Note on Queues

Tokyo has queues. Serious queues. Hour-long queues for ramen, forty-minute queues for particular bakeries, twenty-minute queues for a specific takoyaki stall. Queuing is orderly, quiet, and not considered unusual.

The decision about which queues to join is personal, but a useful principle: the best-known places with the longest queues are usually very good but rarely transcendent. The smaller, less-documented queues — the ones where you cannot find an English review — are where Tokyo's food culture lives most honestly.

Show up. Wait. Eat. Walk to the next one.

---

Tokyo is not a city where you go to have a single memorable meal. It is a city where you eat ten times a day without meaning to, where the quality floor is so high that even the mediocre is better than what most cities offer at their best, and where you return home slightly bewildered by everything else.

The appropriate response to all of it is to start planning to go back.
